Limits

Intuitive Meaning:

The exprerssion $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}f(x)= L$ roughly means:

  1. The input $x$ can be arbitrarly approximated to $a$ (bigger and smaller).
  2. If the input $x\approx a$, then the output $f(x)\approx L$.
    3.
  3. The error in $f(x)\approx L$ can be made as small as desired by making the error in $x\approx a$ small enough (not neccessarily zero).
    4.

As the input $x$ approaches $a$, the output $f(x)$ approaches $L$.

$$\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L$$
$$\displaystyle\lim_{\class{fragment highlight-current-red}{\data{fragment-index=4}{x\to}} \class{fragment highlight-current-green}{\data{fragment-index=2}{a}}}f(x) = \class{fragment highlight-current-blue}{\data{fragment-index=3}{L}} $$

Remember:

  • $a$ is not neccessarily a valid input.
  • $L$ is not neccessarily a valid output.
    •
  • $f(x)$ is defined near $a$ from right and left.
$$\displaystyle\lim_{\class{fragment highlight-green}{\data{fragment-index=2}{x\to a}}}f(x) = \class{fragment highlight-blue}{\data{fragment-index=5}{L}} $$

Moreover:

  • The error in $x\approx a$ equals to the absolute difference between $x$ and $a$. The bound of this error is denoted by $\delta$. So, we write $\big\vert x-a\big\vert<\delta$.
  • The error in $f(x)\approx L$ equals to the absolute difference between $f(x)$ and $L$. Its bound is denoted by $\epsilon$. So, we write $\big\vert f(x)-L\big\vert<\delta$.

Formal Definition:

The expression $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L$ is formally defined as follows:

  • $f(x)$ defined on an open interval about $a$, except possibly at $a$.
    •
  • For every $\epsilon > 0$, there is $\delta > 0$ such that $$\left(0<\big\vert x-a\big\vert<\delta \implies \big\vert f(x)-L\big\vert<\epsilon\right),$$ for every $x$ in the domain of $f$.
    •

  • $\epsilon$ is any desired error for $f(x)\approx L$.

    •

  • $\delta$ is a designated error for $x\approx a$. ($\delta$ depends on the choice of $\epsilon$)

    •

Example:

Prove, by definition, that $$\lim_{x\to 2}\left(2x-1\right)=3.$$

Solution:

Let $\epsilon>0$ be arbitrary (representing any desirable error in the output). We need to find $\delta>0$ such that $$0<\big\vert x-2\big\vert<\delta\implies \big\vert(2x-1)-3\big\vert<\epsilon.$$

Step 1:

Solve the inequality in the right-hand side of $\implies$. $$\begin{aligned}\big\vert(2x-1)-3\big\vert<\epsilon \implies & \big\vert 2x-4\big\vert<\epsilon\\ \implies & \big\vert 2(x-2)\big\vert<\epsilon\\ \implies & 2\cdot \big\vert x-2\big\vert<\epsilon\\ \implies & \big\vert x-2\big\vert<\frac{\epsilon}{2}\\ \end{aligned}$$ The solution set is the open interval that is centered at $2$ and has radius $\displaystyle\frac{\epsilon}{2}$.

Step 2:

Note that $\big\vert x-2\big\vert< \delta$ is the open interval that is centered at $2$ and whose radius is $\delta$. We need to find a suitable radius $\delta$ such that we guarantee that the open interval $\big\vert x-2\big\vert< \delta$ is included completely inside the solution of the inequality in the previuous step. So, we may take $$\displaystyle\delta=\frac{\epsilon}{2}.$$

Step 3:

We need to prove that our choice $\displaystyle\delta=\frac{\epsilon}{2}$ is indeed suitable to guarantee the implication $\implies$. For this aim, let $x$ be any element in the domain of $f$, then $$\begin{aligned} \big\vert x-2\big\vert<\delta \implies & \big\vert x-2\big\vert<\frac{\epsilon}{2}\\ \implies & 2\cdot \big\vert x-2\big\vert<\epsilon\\ \implies & \big\vert 2(x-2)\big\vert<\epsilon\\ \implies & \big\vert 2x-4\big\vert<\epsilon\\ \implies & \big\vert(2x-1)-3\big\vert<\epsilon, \end{aligned}$$ and hence the proof is established.

Example:

For the limit $$\lim_{x\to 5}\sqrt{x-1}=2,$$ find $\delta>0$ that works for $\epsilon=1$.

Solution:

We need to find $\delta>0$ such that $$0<\big\vert x-5\big\vert<\delta\implies \big\vert\sqrt{x-1}-2\big\vert<1.$$

Step 1:

Solve the inequality in the right-hand side of $\implies$. $$\begin{aligned}\big\vert\sqrt{x-1}-2\big\vert<1 \implies & -1< \sqrt{x-1}-2< 1\\ \implies & 1< \sqrt{x-1}< 3\\ \implies & 1< x-1< 9\\ \implies & 2< x < 10\\ \end{aligned}$$ The solution set is the open interval $(2,10)$.

Step 2:

We need to find $\delta>0$ that places the interval $\big\vert x-5\big\vert\leq \delta$ (centered at $5$) inside the interval $(2,10)$. The distance from $5$ to the nearer endpoint of $(2, 10)$ is $3$. If we take $\delta=3$ or any smaller positive number, then the inequality $\big\vert x-5\big\vert<\delta$ will automatically place $x$ between $2$ and $10$.

Step 3:

Let $x$ be any element in the domain of $f$, then $$\begin{aligned} \big\vert x-5\big\vert<\delta \implies & \big\vert x-5\big\vert<3\\ \implies & -3 < x-5 < 3\\ \implies & 2 < x < 8\\ \implies & 2 < x < 10\\ \implies & 1 < x-1 < 9\\ \implies & 1 < \sqrt{x-1} < 3\\ \implies & -1 < \sqrt{x-1}-2 < 1\\ \implies & \big\vert\sqrt{x-1}-2\big\vert<1\\ \end{aligned}$$

Calculating Limits

Theorem: (Limits of Polynomials)

If $P(x) = b_n\cdot x^n + b_{n-1}\cdot x^{n-1} + \cdot + b_0$, then $$\lim_{x\to a}P(x) = b_n\cdot a^n + b_{n-1}\cdot a^{n-1} + \cdot + b_0.$$

So, limits of polynomials can be calculated by direct substitution.

Example:

Evaluate $$\lim_{x\to -1}x^3+4x^2-3.$$

Solution:

$$\lim_{x\to -1}x^3+4x^2-3=(-1)^3+4(-1)^2-3=0.$$

Theorem: (Limits of Rational Functions)

If $P(x)$ and $Q(x)$ are polynomials and $Q(a)\not=0$, then $$\lim_{x\to a}\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}=\frac{P(a)}{Q(a)}.$$

So, limits of rational functions can be calculated by direct substitution if the denominator is not equal to zero.

Example:

Evaluate $$\lim_{x\to -1}\frac{x^3+4x^2-3}{x^2+5}.$$

Solution:

$$\lim_{x\to -1}\frac{x^3+4x^2-3}{x^2+5}=\frac{(-1)^3+4(-1)^2-3}{(-1)^2+5}=\frac{0}{6}=0.$$

If the value of the limit can't be uniquely determined, or if it is not a real number, then the limit does not exist.

$$\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}f(x)= \class{fragment highlight-current-blue}{\data{fragment-index=4}{\text{D.N.E.}}}$$

Example:

Evaluate $$\lim_{x\to 1}\frac{1}{x-1}.$$

Solution:

$$\lim_{x\to 1}\frac{1}{x-1}=\frac{1}{(1)-1}=\frac{1}{0}=\text{undefined}.$$ So, the limit does not exist.

Be Careful:

$$\frac{1}{0}=\text{undefined}$$ $$\frac{0}{0}=\text{indeterminant}$$

Rational Functions with Zero Denominators

If $Q(x)$ is a polynomial, and $Q(a)=0$, then $$Q(x)=(x-a)\cdot R(x)$$ for some polynomial $R(x)$.

So, if the denominator of a rational function is zero, then canceling common factors in the numerator and denominator may reduce the fraction to one whose denominator is no longer zero.

Example:

Evaluate $$\lim_{x\to 1}\frac{x^2+x-2}{x^2-x}.$$

Solution:

$$\lim_{x\to 1}\frac{x^2+x-2}{x^2-x}=\frac{(1)^2+(1)-2}{(1)^2-1}=\frac{0}{0}=\text{indeterminant}$$

$$\begin{aligned} \lim_{x\to 1}\frac{x^2+x-2}{x^2-x}= & \lim_{x\to 1}\frac{(x-1)(x+2)}{x(x-1)}\\ = & \lim_{x\to 1}\frac{\cancel{(x-1)}(x+2)}{x\cancel{(x-1)}}\\ = & \lim_{x\to 1}\frac{(x+2)}{x}\\ = & \frac{(1)+2}{(1)}\\ = & \hspace{0.3cm} 3 \end{aligned}$$ So, the limit exists and is equal to $3$.

Theorem:

If $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L$ and $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}g(x)=M$, then

  1. $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}\left(f(x)\pm g(x)\right)=L\pm M$

  2. $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}\left(f(x)\cdot g(x)\right)=L\cdot M$

  3. $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}\left(\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right)=\frac{L}{M}$, if $M\not=0$

Theorem:

If $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L$ and $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}g(x)=M$, then

  1. $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}\left(k\cdot f(x)\right)=k\cdot L$

  2. $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}\left(f(x)\right)^n=L^n$, $n$ is a positive integer

  3. $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}\sqrt[n]{f(x)}=\sqrt[n]{L}$, $n$ is a positive integer, and if $n$ is even, then we assume that $L>0$

Example:

Evaluate $$\lim_{x\to -2}\sqrt{4x^2-3}.$$

Solution:

$$\lim_{x\to -2}\sqrt{4x^2-3}=\sqrt{4(-2)^2-3}=\sqrt{16-3}=\sqrt{13}.$$

Example:

Evaluate $$\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sqrt{x^2+100}-10}{x^2}.$$

Solution:

$$\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sqrt{x^2+100}-10}{x^2}=\frac{\sqrt{(0)^2+100}-10}{(0)^2}=\frac{0}{0}=ind.$$

$$\begin{aligned} \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sqrt{x^2+100}-10}{x^2}= & \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sqrt{x^2+100}-10}{x^2}\times \frac{\sqrt{x^2+100}+10}{\sqrt{x^2+100}+10}\\ = & \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\left(\sqrt{x^2+100}-10\right)\cdot \left(\sqrt{x^2+100}+10\right)}{x^2\cdot \left(\sqrt{x^2+100}+10\right)}\\ = & \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\left(\sqrt{x^2+100}\right)^2-\left(10\right)^2}{x^2\cdot \left(\sqrt{x^2+100}+10\right)}\\ = & \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\left(x^2+100\right)-100}{x^2\cdot \left(\sqrt{x^2+100}+10\right)}\\ = & \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\left(x^2+\cancel{100}\right)-\cancel{100}}{x^2\cdot \left(\sqrt{x^2+100}+10\right)} \end{aligned}$$

$$\begin{aligned} = & \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{x^2}{x^2\cdot \left(\sqrt{x^2+100}+10\right)}\\ = & \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\cancel{x^2}}{\cancel{x^2}\cdot \left(\sqrt{x^2+100}+10\right)}\\ = & \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2+100}+10}\\ = & \frac{1}{\sqrt{(0)^2+100}+10}\\ = & \hspace{0.3cm} \frac{1}{20} \end{aligned}$$

Example:

Evaluate $$\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\frac{1}{x-1}+\frac{1}{x+1}}{x}.$$

Solution:

$$\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\frac{1}{x-1}+\frac{1}{x+1}}{x}=\frac{\frac{1}{(0)-1}+\frac{1}{(0)+1}}{(0)}=\frac{0}{0}=ind.$$

$$\begin{aligned} \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\frac{1}{x-1}+\frac{1}{x+1}}{x} = & \lim_{x\to 0}\left(\frac{1}{x-1}+\frac{1}{x+1}\right)\cdot \frac{1}{x}\\ = & \lim_{x\to 0}\left(\frac{(x+1)+(x-1)}{(x-1)(x+1)}\right)\cdot \frac{1}{x}\\ = & \lim_{x\to 0}\left(\frac{(x+\cancel{1})+(x-\cancel{1})}{(x-1)(x+1)}\right)\cdot \frac{1}{x}\\ = & \lim_{x\to 0}\left(\frac{2x}{(x-1)(x+1)}\right)\cdot \frac{1}{x}\\ = & \lim_{x\to 0}\left(\frac{2x}{x^2-1}\right)\cdot \frac{1}{x} \end{aligned}$$

$$\begin{aligned} = & \lim_{x\to 0}\left(\frac{2\cancel{x}}{x^2-1}\right)\cdot \frac{1}{\cancel{x}}\\ = & \lim_{x\to 0}\left(\frac{2}{x^2-1}\right)\cdot 1\\ = & \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{2}{x^2-1}\\ = & \frac{2}{(0)^2-1}\\ = & \hspace{0.3cm} -2 \end{aligned}$$

Sandwich Theorem:

Suppose that $g(x)\leq f(x) \leq h(x)$ for all $x$ in some open interval containing $a$, except possibly at $x=a$ itself. If $$\lim_{x\to a}g(x)=\lim_{x\to a}h(x)=L,$$ then $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L$.

$$\lim_{x\to a}h(x)=\lim_{x\to a}g(x)=L \implies \lim_{x\to a}f(x)=L$$

Limit exists:

If the function $f(x)$ is defined near $x=a$ from both sides (left and right), and one of the following is true:

  1. There is no hole and no jumb in the graph at $x=a$.
  2. There is a hole (no jump) in the graph at $x=a$ with another point for a different value of the function at $x=a$.
    3.
  3. There is a hole (no jump) in the graph at $x=a$ with no other point for a value of the function at $x=a$.
    4.

Limit does not exist:

If one (or more) of the following is true:

  1. $f(x)$ is not defined near $x=a$ from one side (right or left).
  2. There is a jump in the graph of $f(x)$ at $x=a$.
  3. $f(x)$ oscillates between two or more values as $x$ approaches $a$.
  4. The graph of $f(x)$ is getting closer to a vertical line as $x$ approaches $a$ from one side (or both).
